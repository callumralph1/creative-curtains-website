The Complete Guide
to Part O.

What Part O of the Building Regulations requires, who it applies to, what products satisfy it, and what the Future Homes Standard means for projects completing after March 2027.

What is Part O?

Part O is Part O of Schedule 1 to the Building Regulations 2010 (England), introduced by SI 2021/1391 and in force from 15 June 2022. It is titled "Overheating" and establishes requirements for limiting unwanted solar gains and providing adequate means of removing heat from new dwellings.

The associated Approved Document O (AD O) provides guidance on how to satisfy Part O. It applies to new dwellings, including extensions that create a new dwelling, student accommodation, care homes and similar residential building types.

Who does it apply to?

Part O applies to:

  • All new dwellings (houses, flats, maisonettes)
  • Student accommodation
  • Care homes and extra care housing
  • Buildings created from material change of use that result in a dwelling

It does not currently apply to commercial buildings, schools or offices, though overheating in these building types is addressed by other means (CIBSE TM52, BB93 etc).

What does it require?

AD O offers two compliance pathways: a simplified method and a dynamic thermal modelling method.

The simplified method involves limiting the total area of glazing on each elevation and ensuring that either passive cooling (cross-ventilation, night purging) or mechanical cooling is present. The methodology varies by dwelling type and location.

The dynamic thermal modelling method requires a full TM59 (residential) or TM52 (non-residential) assessment by a qualified thermal modeller. The building is modelled and must pass the CIBSE overheating criteria.

Do internal blinds satisfy Part O?

No. AD O is unambiguous on this point. Internal blinds, curtains and shutters — regardless of their solar control performance — do not count as solar shading under Part O. Only external solar shading (shading fitted to the outside of the glazing) is credited in the Part O methodology.

This is because internal blinds allow solar radiation to pass through the glass and enter the building as heat before the blind can intercept it. External shading prevents solar radiation from reaching the glass in the first place, which is far more effective.

What external shading satisfies Part O?

Any external shading system that is included in the TM59 thermal model and reduces solar heat gain sufficiently to pass the CIBSE criteria will satisfy Part O. Common compliant systems include:

  • External roller screens (openness factor 1%–10%, g-value typically 0.10–0.25)
  • Adjustable external louvre systems (aluminium aerofoil blades)
  • External Venetian blinds (aluminium slats, 80mm or 100mm)
  • Fixed brise-soleil and louvre panels
  • Overhangs and canopies (fixed geometry, modelled)
  • MicroLouvre® screens (g-value from 0.11)

The key is that the shading must be modelled by a thermal modeller using actual product g-values, and the dwelling must pass the TM59 criteria with the shading in place.

What is TM59?

CIBSE TM59 is the methodology for assessing overheating risk in homes using dynamic thermal modelling software (typically IES-VE or DesignBuilder). The thermal model simulates the dwelling's internal temperatures across a full year using Design Summer Year weather data. The building passes if the percentage of occupied hours exceeding defined temperature thresholds is within the permitted limits.

TM59 requires accurate input data including g-values for all glazing and solar shading products. We provide this data in the format your thermal modeller needs.

The Future Homes Standard

The Future Homes Standard was laid before Parliament on 24 March 2026 and comes into force in March 2027. It significantly tightens the energy performance requirements for new dwellings and is expected to increase the solar shading requirements for compliance.

Specifically, the Future Homes Standard is expected to require lower g-values for glazing and shading systems on south-facing elevations, and is likely to make TM59 modelling the default (rather than the simplified method) for many building types.

We are already specifying to the anticipated Future Homes Standard requirements for any project with a completion date after March 2027.
